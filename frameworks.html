Frameworks

Governance frameworks that make institutional responsibility explicit, survivable, and transferable.

What we mean by frameworks

PublicLogic frameworks are governance structures — not software, methodologies, or change programs.

They define the minimum conditions required for work to be carried safely by an institution rather than by individuals.

Each framework addresses a recurring failure mode: unclear authority, unbounded responsibility, fragmented records, or continuity that depends on people instead of systems.

Why frameworks matter

Public institutions rarely fail from bad intent or lack of effort. They fail when risk is allowed to live in individuals instead of in structure.

When authority is implicit, responsibility unbounded, or work unable to survive turnover, people compensate through vigilance, memory, and personal sacrifice. That compensation is not durable.

PublicLogic frameworks interrupt this pattern by making institutional responsibility explicit, documented, and owned.

VAULT™ — Core Governance Framework

VAULT™ is authority-first and record-first — designed for environments with statutory obligations, public accountability, and staff turnover.

It focuses on six structural domains that determine whether governance holds under pressure:

  • Authority: who can decide, approve, and override, and under what conditions
  • Records: what must exist, where it lives, and who owns it
  • Time: deadlines, duration, and obligation over time
  • Fiscal responsibility: how financial authority and accountability are carried
  • Correction: how errors are identified, addressed, and closed
  • Transfer: how work survives staff and leadership change

VAULT does not prescribe tools or workflows. It defines the minimum structural conditions required for governance to be defensible, auditable, and survivable.

Institutional Stewardship — The Governing Model

Institutional Stewardship is the governance model that PublicLogic is built upon and continues to refine.

It begins with a simple question: before new work enters an organization, has the institution decided where the resulting risk will live?

The model defines four non-negotiable structural conditions that must be resolved before change proceeds:

  • Authority must be explicit, lawful, and documented
  • Responsibility must be bounded and survivable
  • Scope must be explicit and controlled
  • Continuity must not depend on individuals

When these conditions are unmet, institutions unintentionally transfer risk onto staff. Institutional Stewardship exists to prevent that transfer by requiring structural ownership before action.

VAULT is the practical governance framework we developed to address the failure modes Institutional Stewardship makes visible.

This model defines structural conditions, not a change methodology.

How frameworks are used

Frameworks are not delivered as stand-alone products. They are used to:

  • Guide governance design decisions
  • Shape artifacts, records, and interfaces
  • Evaluate whether responsibility is defensible
  • Support training and operational clarity
  • Test whether structures will survive pressure and turnover

Frameworks do not replace local judgment or statutory nuance. They provide a stable lens for making those judgments deliberately rather than implicitly.

What frameworks are not

  • Software platforms
  • Diagnostic products
  • Culture change programs
  • Compliance shortcuts
  • Substitutes for leadership or counsel

Frameworks are successful when they disappear into practice and no longer require explanation.

Relationship to services

Frameworks describe what must be structurally true for governance to be safe.

Services describe how we help institutions make those conditions real.

Not every engagement uses every framework. Frameworks are applied selectively, based on actual risk and institutional conditions.
