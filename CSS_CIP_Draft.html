Summary

A Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) is how towns plan, prioritize, and fund their big-ticket needs: roads, buildings, vehicles, technology, and infrastructure. It’s the bridge between today’s operating budget and tomorrow’s capital reality.

Why is it hard? Because building a CIP means herding dozens of requests, aligning them with financial limits, balancing politics, and producing something staff and boards can actually keep alive year after year. Most communities either never start or let their plan lapse after a year or two.

This partnership is designed to fix that: CSS leads delivery, Public Logic provides the frameworks, and Polimorphic makes the workflows and data entry sustainable. The pilot municipality gets a working CIP they can actually maintain.

What a CIP Really Does

A strong CIP is not just a spreadsheet of projects. It is:

  • A structured intake so every department submits requests the same way
  • A scoring rubric that boards and staff agree on in advance
  • A 5–10 year financial view of debt, pay-go, and grants
  • A pipeline of adoptable budgets and shovel-ready projects
  • Public-facing materials that explain decisions clearly

Without these elements, CIPs collapse under politics or staff workload. With them, towns have a roadmap that can survive elections and transitions.

Partnership Model

  • Capital Strategic Solutions: leads workshops, scoring, affordability reviews, and board packet prep.
  • Public Logic: delivers templates, calendars, playbooks, and standardized processes that travel town to town.
  • Polimorphic: provides the platform — low-code forms, AI-assisted intake, dashboards, and document automation.
  • The Town: owns the data, adopts the plan, and uses the tools to keep it alive year after year.

Process (Repeatable Annual Cycle)

Annual Cycle

  • Fall: project intake and updates
  • Winter: scoring, draft plan, affordability checks
  • Spring: board/committee review and adoption
  • Summer: grant work and project tracking

Outputs

  • Capital policy statement
  • Project one-pagers (scope, cost, funding, O&M)
  • Ranked project list with scoring notes
  • Board packets and public hearing decks
  • Website summaries and FAQs
