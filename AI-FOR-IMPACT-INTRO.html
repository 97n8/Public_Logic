What Municipal Teams Experience Every Day

Work in a town hall rarely arrives as a clearly defined project. It arrives as interruptions. An email that might be a public records request. A phone call that suddenly creates a deadline. A walk-in that becomes an obligation. A forwarded message with "FYI" and no clear next step.

Staff are then expected to determine what the work actually is, who is responsible for it, what rules apply, what timelines matter, and how to bring it to closure. All of this happens while managing everything else.

Example A resident calls about a tree near their property line. The call gets routed to DPW, who determines it's on private property. But the resident mentioned the tree "might be diseased and could fall on the road." Now it's potentially a public safety issue—does that trigger an inspection obligation? Who's responsible for documenting that the town was notified? If the tree falls six months later, what's the record? Currently, this lives in someone's memory or a forwarded email chain.

The issue isn't the tree. It's that nobody knows what the call activated—or whether it activated anything at all.

The result: important obligations often live in inboxes, personal notes, or memory. Follow-through depends on individual judgment and availability rather than shared structure. When someone is out, leaves, or becomes overloaded, work can stall or reappear later under pressure.

This is not a staffing problem. It is a structure problem. The work is real. The pressure is real. What is missing is a system designed to hold it.

How the Pilot Works

AI for Impact is a no-cost pilot delivered jointly by PublicLogic and Polimorphic.

PublicLogic leads the initial review, working with staff to understand current workflows, how work enters the organization, where handoffs occur, and where things tend to become unclear or difficult to track. Nathan Boudreau and Dr. Allison Weiss Rothschild conduct this work together—combining governance design with attention to real human capacity under pressure. This step is practical and collaborative. It focuses on listening, mapping what exists today, and identifying opportunities to make day-to-day work easier to manage and easier to sustain.

PublicLogic's work is included as part of the overall Polimorphic package. It happens first and helps ensure that any technology work is grounded in how the town actually operates.

Polimorphic supports implementation only after this structure is in place, translating agreed-upon workflows into well-defined, supported systems.

A Note on AI

This pilot uses AI tools carefully and transparently. Any automation is implemented only after human governance structures are clear—staff will always know what's automated, what remains human judgment, and how to override automated steps when circumstances require it. AI supports the work; it does not replace judgment or operate beyond staff visibility.

Every participating community receives a custom summary report that reflects what was learned, highlights key pressure points, and outlines practical options for improvement. Communities keep this report whether or not they choose to move forward.

Comparable reviews and workflow assessments are typically procured as paid consulting engagements, often budgeted in the mid five-figure range. As part of this pilot, the full review and summary report are provided at no cost to participating communities.

There is no obligation, no sales pressure, and no catch.

Key Terms (Plain Language)

VAULT
PublicLogic's governance framework. It provides clear structure for who decides, who is responsible, how work moves, and how it is documented so it holds up over time.
CASE
A structured workspace for a specific piece of municipal work. Each CASE has clear ownership, visible status, required documentation, and a clear end point, consistent with applicable public records and retention requirements.
Kismet
The forms, templates, and automated steps created during the pilot. These are town-owned, non-proprietary tools that support daily operations and can be reused and updated over time.

What Changes for Staff

After a workflow is restructured through the pilot, staff no longer need to decide on the fly whether something "counts," who owns it, or whether silence represents approval or risk.

Those questions are answered by design. Staff can focus on the substance of the work instead of carrying the administrative uncertainty around it.

Clear structure also changes who can do the work. When obligations, deadlines, and handoffs are visible and systematic rather than held in memory or tribal knowledge, municipalities can more confidently rely on part-time staff, trained volunteers, and employees with disabilities—people who may have struggled in "wild west" operations but thrive when expectations are explicit and consistent. This expands the available workforce while improving reporting and accountability across the board.
